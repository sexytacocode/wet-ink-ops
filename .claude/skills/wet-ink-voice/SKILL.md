# Wet Ink Magazine — Editorial Voice Guide
 
## What Is Wet Ink?
 
Wet Ink Magazine is a print and digital publication covering the adult entertainment industry with the authority, depth, and editorial seriousness that the industry has always deserved and rarely received. It was founded by Holly Randall — photographer, director, and host of *Holly Randall Unfiltered* — and is built on the conviction that the people, culture, and business of adult entertainment merit sophisticated, intelligent coverage. Wet Ink is not a trade sheet. It is not a fan site. It is a magazine.
 
---
 
## The Voice
 
The Wet Ink voice is **authoritative, insider, sharp, and dry**. It writes about the adult industry the way a great culture magazine writes about film or music — with expertise, without apology, and with genuine critical curiosity. It does not sensationalize. It does not sanitize. It does not moralize.
 
The voice belongs to someone who has been in this industry for decades, knows everyone, has seen everything, and still finds it genuinely interesting.
 
---
 
## Core Principles
 
### 1. Authority Without Arrogance
Write from deep insider knowledge. Assume the reader is intelligent and curious, not shocked or titillated. You are not explaining porn to a stranger — you are talking to someone who either works in the industry or respects it enough to read a serious magazine about it.
 
### 2. Critical of Stigma, Not Preachy About It
Wet Ink pushes back on mainstream media's distorted, moralizing coverage of sex work — but it does so through sharp observation and good writing, not lectures. Make the point and move on. Trust the reader.
 
### 3. Dry Humor
The voice has a bone-dry wit. Jokes land through understatement, timing, and the unexpected aside — not exclamation points or winking puns. If something is funny, let it be funny. Don't announce it.
 
### 4. Flowing Prose
Wet Ink does not write in bullet points, listicles, or short choppy fragments. Sentences connect. Paragraphs build. Ideas develop across multiple sentences before landing. This is a magazine, not a content farm.
 
### 5. Specificity Over Generality
Name the scene. Name the performer. Name the film. Name the festival. Vague gesturing at "the industry" is a crutch. Specific detail is what gives writing authority.

## What to Avoid
 
These are the primary signals that a piece has NOT been written in Wet Ink voice. Eliminate all of them.
 
- **Excessive em dashes** — one or two per piece is fine; three or more is a tic
- **Short, choppy sentences.** Like this. One idea. Then another. It reads like AI.
- **Throat-clearing openers** — "In a world where..." / "It's no secret that..." / "Now more than ever..."
- **Hedging language** — "seems to," "appears to be," "arguably" used as a shield rather than a genuine qualifier
- **Moralizing** — do not editorialize about whether something is good or bad for the industry unless the piece is explicitly an opinion piece
- **Faux-neutral both-sidesing** — Wet Ink has a point of view. Use it.
- **Redundant enthusiasm** — "incredibly," "truly," "really," "so" as intensifiers
- **Explaining the obvious** — trust the reader
- **Overly formal transitions** — "Furthermore," "In conclusion," "It is worth noting that"

## Tone by Content Type
 
### Performer Profiles
Cinematic, specific, empathetic without being hagiographic. Find the detail that makes the person real — the unexpected job they had before, the scene that defined their reputation, the thing they believe about sex that most people misunderstand. Build toward that detail; don't lead with it.
 
### Industry News Rewrites (from AVN, XBIZ, etc.)
Strip out the press release language. Rewrite in active, declarative sentences. Add context where Wet Ink's insider knowledge is relevant. Keep it tight.
 
### Opinion / Cultural Commentary
This is where the voice can be most pointed. Take a clear position. Use humor. Do not soften the argument for readers who might disagree.
 
### SEO / AEO Content
Even search-optimized content should sound like Wet Ink. Use natural-language subheadings (questions work well for AEO). Write FAQ sections in the same voice — confident, specific, not robotic. Do not let SEO requirements flatten the prose into keyword soup.
 
### Podcast-to-Article
When converting *Holly Randall Unfiltered* episodes to editorial, preserve the specific details and quotes from the conversation, but restructure for reading rather than listening. The article should feel reported, not transcribed.

## Quick Checklist Before Submitting
 
- [ ] No more than two em dashes in the entire piece
- [ ] No sentence under eight words that isn't doing specific intentional work
- [ ] No opener that could apply to any article about any topic
- [ ] At least one specific proper noun (name, title, place) per paragraph
- [ ] Humor lands through understatement, not announcement
- [ ] No hedging language used as a shield
- [ ] Reads like a person wrote it, not a content brief
